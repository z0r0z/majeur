### HIGH-1: castVote lacks proposal state check, allowing votes after Succeeded/Queued state to reverse outcomes and manipulate futarchy

**Contract:** `src/Moloch.sol`

**Description**

The `castVote()` function in `Moloch.sol` lacks a state check to ensure the proposal is in the Active state before accepting votes (L347-392). The function validates `executed[id]`, TTL expiry, and futarchy resolution status, but never calls `state(id)` to confirm the proposal has not already transitioned to Succeeded, Queued, Defeated, Expired, or Executed. After a proposal reaches Succeeded and is queued (when `timelockDelay > 0`), any unvoted shareholder can call `castVote()` with `support=0` (AGAINST) during the timelock window. If sufficient AGAINST weight accumulates to make `againstVotes >= forVotes`, the `state()` function will re-evaluate to Defeated once the timelock elapses, blocking execution. This violates vote finality and enables futarchy exploitation: an attacker can split voting power across accounts, vote YES with small accounts to pass quorum and trigger auto-futarchy funding, queue the proposal, then cast larger AGAINST votes from other accounts to flip the outcome and claim the futarchy pool as the AGAINST winner.

Impact: An attacker can reverse the outcome of a proposal after it has reached Succeeded state and been queued for execution. By accumulating sufficient AGAINST votes during the timelock window, the attacker can cause the proposal to transition to Defeated, blocking execution. In futarchy-enabled proposals, this enables the attacker to manipulate the voting outcome, queue the proposal with YES votes, then flip it to NO and claim the entire futarchy reward pool intended for YES voters. This violates the intended vote finality after Succeeded state and results in theft of futarchy funds from legitimate YES-side participants.

### MED-1: Front-run openProposal to hijack proposerOf and immediately cancel governance proposals

**Contract:** `src/Moloch.sol`

**Description**

The `openProposal()` function in `Moloch.sol` assigns `proposerOf[id] = msg.sender` (L300) without checking whether the proposal has already been opened. An attacker with sufficient `proposalThreshold` voting power can front-run the legitimate proposer's `openProposal()` call (or the auto-open triggered by the first `castVote()`) by extracting the proposal id from the mempool. The attacker calls `openProposal(id)` to become `proposerOf[id]`, then immediately calls `cancelProposal(id)` in the same transaction via `multicall()`. Since no votes have been cast and (if autoFutarchy is not configured) no futarchy pool exists, `cancelProposal()` succeeds, setting `executed[id] = true` and permanently killing the proposal. The legitimate proposer must re-submit with a different nonce, and the attack can be repeated indefinitely at minimal gas cost.

Impact: An attacker can persistently block governance proposals by hijacking the proposer role and immediately cancelling proposals before voting begins. This creates a denial-of-service attack on DAOs without autoFutarchy enabled, as the attacker can cancel any proposal with only gas costs as the barrier to entry. Legitimate proposers must repeatedly re-submit proposals with different nonces, degrading governance responsiveness and creating operational friction.

### MED-2: Auto-futarchy over-commits DAO token balance across concurrent proposals when rewardToken is shares or loot

**Contract:** `src/Moloch.sol`

**Description**

The auto-futarchy logic in `openProposal()` computes the amount to earmark based on the DAO's current token balance (L329-333) and increments `F.pool` (L336), but does not transfer or lock the tokens (L305-341). When multiple proposals are opened in sequence, each reads the same DAO balance and each commits up to the full balance to its futarchy pool. For example, with 100 DAO-held shares and two proposals opened, both proposals receive `F.pool = 100`, totaling 200 shares committed against 100 actual. When the first proposal's winning side calls `cashOutFutarchy()`, the `safeTransfer()` call drains the DAO's balance. Subsequent proposal winners calling `cashOutFutarchy()` will have `safeTransfer()` revert due to insufficient balance, permanently unable to claim their rightful rewards.

Impact: Multiple futarchy pools can be over-committed against the DAO's actual token balance, causing later winners to be unable to claim their rewards. When the first winning side cashes out, they drain the DAO's balance. Subsequent proposal winners are left unable to execute `cashOutFutarchy()` because the required payout exceeds the remaining balance, resulting in permanent loss of their futarchy rewards. This affects all proposals after the first one to resolve, creating a systematic denial of payout for later winners.

### MED-3: fundFutarchy omits executed[id] check, enabling permanent fund locking on cancelled proposals

**Contract:** `src/Moloch.sol`

**Description**

The `fundFutarchy()` function in `Moloch.sol` checks only `F.resolved` (L540) but omits a check for `executed[id]` (L530-571). Impact: A funder can permanently lose access to their futarchy contribution on a cancelled proposal. The futarchy accounting is broken because neither resolution path succeeds: the proposal cannot be executed (blocked by `executed[id]`), and the futarchy pool cannot be resolved or cashed out. While the ETH technically remains in the DAO's general balance, the funder receives no return on their futarchy contribution.
